Exploring the role of microbes in mammalian physiology

We are born coated in microbes, we live immersed in microbes, and eventually we die only to be recycled by microbes. Microbes make up a non-trivial proportion of our bodies: somewhere in the range of 2.5 lb for the average human. In short, microbes are an intrinsic part of what it means to be human.

The microbiome extends the human gene repertoire by a factor of ~100 bringing new possibilities for interacting with our environment. My particular interests lie in how microbes affect xenobiotics (drugs and dietary toxins) through their unique pathways of metabolism, and their influence on human pathways. I hope to harness this basic science for personalized medicine approaches including through probiotics.

Despite being more of a wet lab scientist than a computational biologist, I also have a particular interest in how microbiome data is handled and analyzed. As a newer field, many would be surprised to learn that many of the common analysis techniques are contentious and that there is an utter lack of transparency that can have serious ramifications on conclusions that are showing up on news outlets around the world on a seemingly daily basis. I am continuously experimenting with new approaches and will include it them on my github.
